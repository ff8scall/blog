## Executive Summary
- Intel plans to keep its 2026 capital expenditure (capex) flat year-over-year, prioritizing fiscal health and operational discipline.
- The strategy emphasizes filling existing 'shell space' with advanced production tools rather than initiating new physical construction.
- This measured approach is designed to meet AI-driven demand through productivity gains while managing the financial transition of the IDM 2.0 model.

## Strategic Deep-Dive

Intel has signaled a pivotal shift in its financial and operational strategy for the 2026 fiscal year, electing to keep its capital expenditure (capex) broadly flat relative to the previous year. This decision marks a transition from a phase of aggressive physical expansion to one of rigorous capital discipline and operational refinement under the IDM 2.0 framework. For the past several years, Intel has invested heavily in creating 'shell space'—the physical infrastructure of a semiconductor fab—across multiple global sites.

With much of that foundational infrastructure now in place, the company is shifting its spending toward the acquisition of high-output equipment and productivity-enhancing tools. Management believes that the current floor space is sufficient to meet projected demand, provided it is equipped with the latest lithography and testing technologies. This 'efficiency first' approach is designed to maximize the return on invested capital (ROIC) by driving higher throughput and yield (수율) from existing assets.

In the context of the burgeoning AI market, Intel's strategy is to increase capacity density rather than physical footprint. By focusing on the tools that directly translate into shippable silicon, Intel can remain agile in response to fluctuating market demands without the multi-year lead times and massive sunk costs associated with greenfield construction. Furthermore, this flat capex profile is a strategic necessity to maintain a healthy balance sheet while the company scales its Intel Foundry Services (IFS).

Investors are increasingly looking for signs of fiscal prudence, and Intel’s commitment to optimizing factory space utilization over expansionary spending serves as a pragmatic signal. The goal is to achieve a balanced ecosystem where internal product cycles and external foundry orders are synchronized to achieve maximum fab utilization. As the 2026 roadmap unfolds, Intel's success will be measured by its ability to extract more value from every square foot of its global manufacturing network, proving that in the semiconductor industry, productivity can be just as powerful as physical scale.
