**SUBJECT:** Mitigation of Computational Over-Reliance in 2026 Hybrid Specifications

---

### 1. Executive Summary
The transition to the 2026 Formula 1 technical regulations introduces a fundamental shift in Power Unit (PU) philosophy: a move toward a 50/50 split between internal combustion and electrical power. However, preliminary testing and simulations have identified a critical strategic risk: the "algorithmic takeover" of kinetic energy management. Current iterations suggest that on-track acceleration curves are being dictated by autonomous optimization algorithms rather than driver inputs, threatening the sport’s core value proposition of human-centric racing.

### 2. The Core Technical Conflict
The 2026 regulations mandate a significant increase in electrical output (up to 350kW). To manage this increased energy density, engineers have relied heavily on complex software maps to handle energy deployment and harvest.

*   **The Issue:** The software is currently performing "driver-in-the-loop" optimization in real-time. Instead of the driver modulating the throttle to manage traction and energy efficiency, the control systems are overriding driver inputs to ensure the power unit operates within its optimal efficiency window.
*   **The Consequence:** This creates a disconnect between the driver's intent and the car’s kinetic output. If a driver applies throttle, the algorithm—not the driver—determines the acceleration ramp, effectively turning the driver into a passenger of a pre-programmed performance curve.

### 3. Strategic Implications for 2026
This technological trend presents three primary risks to the 2026 competitive landscape:

1.  **Erosion of Driver Agency:** The technical report highlights that if acceleration becomes a function of code rather than skill, the "human element"—the traditional differentiator in F1—will be marginalized. 
2.  **Predictability and "Rail-Track" Performance:** When algorithms govern power delivery to maximize efficiency, the variations in corner exits—typically the primary overtaking window—become standardized. This risks turning races into static, simulation-perfect processions.
3.  **Regulatory Backlash:** The governing body (FIA) is under pressure to rectify this before the 2026 freeze. Failure to enforce "driver-direct" power management could lead to public perception that the sport has transitioned into a software engineering competition rather than a driving discipline.

### 4. Proposed Mitigation Strategies
To address these concerns, the technical roadmap must pivot toward the following:

*   **Restricted Torque Mapping:** Implementing strict regulations on the "Electronic Control Unit (ECU) influence." The regulations must mandate that the electrical power delivery remains directly proportional to the physical position of the throttle pedal, rather than being "smoothed" or "optimized" by the engine mapping.
*   **Decoupling Optimization from Delivery:** Engineering efforts should focus on allowing teams to optimize *efficiency* during the off-throttle or braking phases (harvesting), while requiring the *deployment* phase to be a raw reflection of driver demand.
*   **Revised Testing Protocols:** Introducing "Human-in-the-Loop" validation during the late-stage simulation phases to ensure that acceleration curves are physically reproducible by a human driver without algorithmic interference.

### 5. Final Assessment
The 2026 regulations represent the most significant powertrain overhaul in a decade. While the technical goals of high-hybridization are environmentally sound, the current path risks compromising the essential "racer’s feedback loop." We must prioritize the development of hardware-limited, rather than software-governed, power delivery systems to maintain the integrity of the sport.
