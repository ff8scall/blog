## Japan Airlines (JAL)'s Contactless Revolution: Facial Recognition-Based "Seamless Travel"

As of 2026, Japan Airlines (JAL) has fully implemented "Face Express," a facial recognition boarding system, on all international routes departing from Narita and Haneda airports. This completely replaces the existing cumbersome procedures that required passengers to present their passports and boarding passes multiple times, ushering in an era where passengers' biometric information becomes their digital ID. Built upon SITA's "Smart Path" technology and the "OneID" framework, this system converts facial information registered during check-in into an encrypted token, allowing passengers to pass through baggage drop-off, security checkpoints, and boarding gates without stopping even once.

This represents the pinnacle of Japan's ongoing Airport DX (Digital Transformation) efforts and is establishing itself as a new standard for global air services.

## Achievements of Airport DX and the Challenge of Privacy Protection

The most significant achievement of introducing facial recognition technology is the maximization of operational efficiency. The automated identity verification system is much faster and more accurate than manual comparison by personnel and is becoming a key solution to Japan's chronic airport labor shortage problem. However, behind this convenience lies concern about entering a "surveillance society" and the risk of biometric data leaks.

Because facial information is unique and unchangeable sensitive information, security incidents can lead to catastrophic consequences. To address this, JAL is implementing a strict policy of deleting all biometric data within 24 hours after flight completion, but securing transparency in the collection and utilization of data remains a significant challenge to be addressed.

### KOR_INSIGHT

For facial recognition-based seamless travel to truly succeed, trust in "data sovereignty" must precede technological advancement. An independent monitoring system is needed to prevent biometric information from becoming a means of control by national institutions or airlines. Users must be guaranteed the clear right to know when and how their information is deleted.

Furthermore, as cyberattacks become more sophisticated, hashing and distributed storage technologies for biometric data must be further strengthened. Japan's Airport DX model will gain sustainable competitiveness in the global market only when the value of convenience does not infringe upon the fundamental right to privacy.
