## Vietnam's Energy Policy U-Turn

Vietnam's nuclear power program, suspended in 2016 due to economic burden and safety concerns, is making a dramatic comeback in 2026. The Vietnamese government's decision to restart nuclear power stems from unprecedented power shortages caused by explosive manufacturing growth and the attraction of artificial intelligence (AI) data centers. To achieve its 2050 carbon neutrality target while meeting rapidly increasing electricity demand, Vietnam has repositioned nuclear energy as a core pillar of its national energy security.

In this process, South Korea has emerged as Vietnam's most trusted partner.

## Competitiveness of Korean Nuclear Power Technology

Led by Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power (KHNP), "Team Korea" has gained a dominant position in the Vietnamese nuclear power market due to its world-class adherence to construction deadlines and cost-effectiveness demonstrated at the Barakah Nuclear Plant in the United Arab Emirates (UAE). Vietnam is showing great interest not only in Korea's APR-1400 large-scale reactors, but also in the introduction of Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), which can be deployed in a distributed manner near industrial complexes. South Korea outmaneuvered competitors such as Russia and China by offering a 'total package' encompassing financial support, training of operating personnel, and radioactive waste management, going beyond mere technology supply.

## Geopolitical and Economic Implications

This nuclear power cooperation will elevate the relationship between Korea and Vietnam from 'economic partners' to 'strategic security partners'. Vietnam seeks to avoid dependence on specific major powers by diversifying its energy technology reliance, while Korea will establish a foothold for entering the entire Southeast Asian nuclear power market. This is a Win-Win strategy for both countries to expand their geopolitical influence.

**INSIGHT:**

The ultimate success of the Vietnamese nuclear power project hinges on 'financial risk management' and 'the development of local regulations'. Since Vietnam has no prior experience in nuclear power operation, Korea must go beyond the role of a mere constructor and act as an 'institutional mentor,' co-creating Vietnamese nuclear regulations and safety standards. Furthermore, Korea's diplomatic capabilities will be tested in forming an international financial consortium to raise the enormous construction costs.
