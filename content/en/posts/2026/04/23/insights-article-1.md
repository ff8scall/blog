## Strategic Deep-Dive

### Chinese Companies' Nuclear Fusion Transformation: Chery and Meituan's Strategic Positioning

In April 2026, the entry of Chinese automaker Chery and delivery platform giant Meituan into nuclear fusion research is more than just an investment; it's part of a national energy independence strategy. Nuclear fusion technology, which used to be confined to state-led large-scale science projects, is now being incorporated into the core of private companies' business models. In particular, Chery Automotive, a leader in the electric vehicle market, is seeking to transform itself from a manufacturer of mere transportation into an 'energy company' that directly produces and manages electricity.

This is interpreted as an attempt to secure the fundamental energy source, nuclear fusion, in the future mobility ecosystem where electric vehicles and energy storage systems (ESS) are integrated, thereby completing the final puzzle piece of vertical integration.

### Combining Platform Infrastructure and Energy Security

Meituan's foray into nuclear fusion is a pragmatic choice to secure the sustainability of its infrastructure operations. Millions of self-driving delivery robots and drones, and the massive data centers needed to control them, consume enormous amounts of electricity. Meituan aims to eliminate energy price fluctuation risks and dramatically lower long-term operating costs through nuclear fusion, the "dream energy." This is in line with Western big tech companies collaborating with nuclear and nuclear fusion startups, but China differentiates itself by combining manufacturing capabilities and the platform economy to build a more aggressive commercialization model.

Ultimately, this move is aligned with the Chinese government's geopolitical goal of securing 'energy sovereignty,' becoming a new front in the technology hegemony competition.

### KOR_INSIGHT

Chery and Meituan's nuclear fusion investments are more about 'strategic preemption' than technical feasibility. Commercialization of nuclear fusion still requires decades, and the huge barriers of miniaturization and economic viability of Tokamak devices remain. However, the influx of massive capital and data from automotive and platform companies into nuclear fusion research is likely to dramatically shorten the existing research speed.

Korean companies should also proactively respond to future supply chain risks by securing stakes in next-generation energy production technologies and engaging in technological cooperation, rather than simply remaining energy consumers.
