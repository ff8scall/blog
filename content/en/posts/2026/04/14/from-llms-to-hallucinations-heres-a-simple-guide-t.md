### 1. Executive Summary
The rapid acceleration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) integration across industries has introduced a complex vocabulary that often acts as a barrier to entry for stakeholders. This report synthesizes the foundational terms and concepts identified by TechCrunch, categorizing them into functional groups to provide a clear understanding of the current AI landscape. The objective is to demystify the "black box" of AI through precise technical definitions and contextual applications.

### 2. Foundational Architectures
The current AI boom is primarily driven by specific architectures that allow machines to process and generate human-like data.

*   **Large Language Models (LLMs):** These are the engines behind tools like ChatGPT. An LLM is a type of AI trained on massive datasets (books, articles, code) to recognize, summarize, translate, and predict text.
*   **Generative AI (GenAI):** A broad category of AI capable of creating new content—including text, images, audio, and video—rather than simply classifying or analyzing existing data.
*   **Transformers:** The specific neural network architecture that revolutionized the field. Transformers use a "self-attention" mechanism to weight the significance of different parts of input data, allowing the model to understand context and long-range dependencies in language.

### 3. Operational Mechanics: How AI "Thinks"
To understand AI performance, one must understand the internal processes that occur between input and output.

*   **Parameters:** These represent the internal variables the model learns from training data. Generally, the more parameters a model has (e.g., GPT-4’s rumored trillions), the more complex the patterns it can recognize.
*   **Tokens:** AI does not read words in the traditional sense; it processes "tokens." A token can be a single character, a syllable, or a whole word. This is the basic unit of text processing.
*   **Training vs. Inference:** 
    *   **Training** is the computationally expensive phase where a model learns from a dataset.
    *   **Inference** is the act of using that trained model to provide an answer or generate content based on a new prompt.

### 4. Behavioral Phenomena and Challenges
As AI models interact with the real world, several unique behaviors and flaws have emerged.

*   **Hallucination:** This is perhaps the most critical term for users to understand. A hallucination occurs when an AI generates a response that is grammatically correct and confident but factually incorrect or nonsensical. This happens because LLMs predict the "next likely word" rather than retrieving facts from a database.
*   **Prompt Engineering:** The emerging discipline of refining the input (the "prompt") to get the most accurate or creative output from an AI.
*   **Fine-Tuning:** The process of taking a pre-trained model and training it further on a smaller, specific dataset (e.g., legal documents or medical records) to make it an expert in a niche field.

### 5. Ethics, Safety, and Alignment
As AI becomes more autonomous, the industry has focused on ensuring these systems remain beneficial to humanity.

*   **Alignment:** The process of ensuring an AI’s goals and behaviors match human values and intentions. A "misaligned" AI might complete a task in a way that is technically correct but harmful or unethical.
*   **Bias:** Because AI models are trained on human-generated data, they often inherit and amplify societal prejudices. Addressing bias is a primary focus of AI safety research.
*   **Black Box:** A term used to describe the lack of transparency in how deep learning models reach specific decisions. Even the creators often cannot explain exactly why a model chose one word over another.

### 6. Conclusion
The transition from specialized academic research to mainstream utility has made AI literacy essential. Understanding the distinction between a model’s training and its inference, or recognizing the inherent risk of hallucinations, allows organizations to deploy these tools more effectively and ethically. As the technology evolves, this lexicon will likely expand, but the foundational terms outlined here remain the bedrock of the modern AI era.
