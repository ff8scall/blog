## Executive Summary
- NASA is heavily reliant on a single provider for lunar spacesuits after competitors like Collins Aerospace exited the race.
* Technical hurdles in managing lunar regolith and thermal control pose critical delay risks.
* This "single-provider" bottleneck highlights a systemic vulnerability in the broader Space Economy infrastructure.

## Strategic Deep-Dive

The success of NASA’s Artemis program is currently tethered to a precarious thread: the development of next-generation Extravehicular Mobility Units (EMUs). After the exit of major defense contractors like Collins Aerospace, NASA has found itself in a "single-provider" trap with Axiom Space. This departure from the traditional redundant procurement model creates a massive single point of failure.

If the remaining provider encounters a significant technical roadblock—such as managing the abrasive lunar regolith or the extreme -200°C temperatures of the lunar south pole—there is no backup plan.

From an industry perspective, this highlights a fragility in the Space Economy. Developing a spacesuit is not merely about garment design; it is about creating a wearable, pressurized spacecraft. The niche expertise required for this means that when one vendor fails or withdraws due to budget constraints, the national timeline for space exploration is jeopardized.

This situation calls for a rethink of how NASA incentivizes competition among mid-sized aerospace firms to ensure that "Silicon Sovereignty" extends into orbit and onto the lunar surface, preventing a monopoly that could delay humanity's return to the Moon by a decade.
